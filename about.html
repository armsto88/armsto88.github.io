Work

How I work on projects

1. Define the ecological question

Start by clarifying what decision the monitoring needs to support, and what signal is actually required to answer it well.

2. Design for field constraints

Plan around power, weather, servicing cadence, and deployment logistics so systems survive real environments, not just test benches.

3. Build and iterate quickly

Prototype, deploy, observe failure points, and improve in short cycles rather than over-optimising before first deployment.

4. Make data usable

Build workflows and interfaces that convert raw observations into outputs ecologists can interpret and use with confidence.

5. Keep systems maintainable

Prefer clear, robust setups over fragile complexity so teams can run and maintain tools without specialist support.

6. Bridge ecology and engineering

Translate between field priorities and technical tradeoffs so both ecological usefulness and technical quality stay aligned.

Philosophy

How I think

  • Practical over flashy. If it can't be deployed in the field, it's not a solution.
  • Field-ready over theoretically elegant. Real constraints produce better design.
  • Systems thinking. Most problems in conservation technology are integration problems, not component problems.
  • Ecological usefulness is the benchmark. Not technical sophistication, not novelty.
  • Interdisciplinary by necessity. Complex problems don't respect disciplinary boundaries.
  • Communication is part of good technical work. A tool nobody can interpret is a tool nobody uses.
  • Curiosity and iteration matter more than having the right answer first.
  • Simplicity is usually harder to build than complexity â€” and usually more valuable.

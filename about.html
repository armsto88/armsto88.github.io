About

Ecologist. Builder.
Conservation technologist.

I'm an ecologist who builds practical tools. My work sits at the intersection of field ecology, environmental technology, eco-acoustics, and data interpretation — with a focus on systems that are actually useful in the field.

Background

How I got here

I came to conservation technology through ecology. My background is in zoology and ecology — time spent in the field, learning how species behave, how ecosystems function, and how difficult it can be to gather reliable data in complex, unpredictable environments.

That field experience gave me a healthy scepticism of technology that looks good in a demo but fails when deployed in the rain at 3am. It also gave me a clear sense of what ecological teams actually need: tools that are simple, reliable, and interpretable.

Over time, I became increasingly drawn to the technical side — acoustic monitoring, remote sensing, environmental sensing hardware, and the data workflows that connect raw field observations to actionable ecological insight. I'm now based in Germany, working across conservation technology projects with an international scope.

The thread running through all of it is the same: building things that help people understand living systems better.

Work

Philosophy

How I think

  • Practical over flashy. If it can't be deployed in the field, it's not a solution.
  • Field-ready over theoretically elegant. Real constraints produce better design.
  • Systems thinking. Most problems in conservation technology are integration problems, not component problems.
  • Ecological usefulness is the benchmark. Not technical sophistication, not novelty.
  • Interdisciplinary by necessity. Complex problems don't respect disciplinary boundaries.
  • Communication is part of good technical work. A tool nobody can interpret is a tool nobody uses.
  • Curiosity and iteration matter more than having the right answer first.
  • Simplicity is usually harder to build than complexity — and usually more valuable.

Outside the work

The human part

I'm an enthusiastic birder and wildlife photographer, with a particular interest in natural soundscapes and acoustic environments. A lot of my interest in eco-acoustics comes directly from the experience of listening carefully to wild places — trying to understand what sound can tell you about an ecosystem that visual observation can't.

I'm motivated by building tools that help people hear, see, and understand living systems more clearly. That's what pulls everything together for me.
